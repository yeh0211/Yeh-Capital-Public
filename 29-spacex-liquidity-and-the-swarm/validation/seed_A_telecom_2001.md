# Telecom sector state report — as of 30 June 2001
All information below reflects what was publicly knowable on 30 June 2001. Nothing later.

## The demand layer: carriers and the bandwidth buildout
- The late-1990s internet buildout was financed by carrier debt. Long-haul and competitive local carriers (WorldCom, Global Crossing, Qwest, Level 3, Williams, and dozens of CLECs) borrowed heavily to lay fiber on the thesis that internet traffic doubles every hundred days.
- Most of the fiber that has been laid remains unlit. Wholesale bandwidth prices are falling steeply. Carrier revenue growth is slowing while debt service is fixed.
- WorldCom is the second-largest US long-distance carrier, built through serial acquisition (MCI). Its stock has fallen hard from its 1999 peak; its reported margins depend on line-cost ratios that analysts struggle to reconcile with peers; its CEO has personal loans secured by company stock. Capex guidance is being cut.
- Global Crossing carries roughly $12B of debt against collapsing wholesale prices. Winstar Communications, a fixed-wireless CLEC, filed for bankruptcy in April 2001 and is suing its main equipment vendor.
- US carrier capital spending, which roughly doubled between 1996 and 2000, is now being cut quarter after quarter. Estimates suggest aggregate carrier capex could fall by half over the next two years if revenue does not recover.

## Tier-1: the equipment vendors
- Lucent Technologies (FY2000 revenue roughly $30B) is in visible distress: revenue is falling fast, it has drawn down credit lines, announced mass layoffs, is divesting units (Agere IPO), and replaced its CEO. Central problem: VENDOR FINANCING — Lucent extended billions of dollars of credit to its own customers (CLECs and carriers) to buy its equipment. Winstar's April 2001 bankruptcy left Lucent holding roughly $700M of exposure to one customer alone. Total customer-financing commitments run to several billions.
- Nortel Networks (2000 revenue roughly $30B) issued one of the largest guidance collapses in corporate history in early 2001: Q1 revenue far below plan, a multi-billion writedown of acquisitions, tens of thousands of layoffs announced. Nortel also carries large vendor-financing commitments to carriers. Its stock is down more than 80% from its 2000 peak, when it briefly represented over a third of the value of the Toronto exchange.
- Cisco Systems (FY2000 revenue roughly $19B, the healthiest balance sheet of the three) took a $2.25 billion inventory write-off in the April 2001 quarter — components and finished goods bought for demand that did not materialize — and cut 8,500 jobs. Cisco carries comparatively little vendor financing and generates positive free cash flow. Its stock is down roughly 75% from the March 2000 peak.
- All three vendors over-ordered components through 1999-2000 because of shortages ("double ordering"); the channel is now stuffed with inventory.

## Tier-2: the component suppliers
- JDS Uniphase is the dominant optical-component supplier (lasers, amplifiers, filters) to Lucent, Nortel, Alcatel, Ciena. Fiscal-2001 revenue (ending June 2001) is roughly $3.2B, built by acquisition during the bubble: it paid for SDL, E-Tek and others largely in stock at peak prices, leaving tens of billions of goodwill on its balance sheet. Customers are cancelling orders and burning their own inventory before buying new components. JDSU has announced layoffs and warned on revenue.
- Corning (optical fiber), PMC-Sierra and Vitesse (telecom chips), and the contract manufacturers (Solectron, Flextronics) all expanded capacity into the 2000 peak and sell predominantly to the tier-1 vendors and carriers.

## Structure of the system
- The carriers owe the banks and bondholders. The vendors financed the weakest carriers and CLECs themselves. The component suppliers depend on a handful of tier-1 vendors for most of their revenue. Component demand is therefore carrier capex passed through two layers of inventory.
- Interest rates: the Federal Reserve has been cutting aggressively since January 2001. The broader economy is slowing; the Nasdaq is down more than 60% from its peak.

## Prediction request
Simulate the next eight quarters (Q3 2001 through Q2 2003) for this system. Questions:
1. Does a major carrier fail? Which kind, and through what mechanism?
2. What happens to each tier-1 vendor (Lucent, Nortel, Cisco) — revenue trajectory, survival, and why do their paths differ?
3. What happens to the component tier (JDS Uniphase and peers) relative to the tier-1 vendors — does it fall less, the same, or more, and why?
4. Where do the largest balance-sheet losses ultimately appear (writedowns, defaults, uncollected financing)?
