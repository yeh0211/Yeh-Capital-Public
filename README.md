# yeh-capital — research notes

Independent quant and markets research by **Hsin Cheng Yeh**. Each note is a self-contained study: a hypothesis I formed, the data and method, the result (including when the result is *no effect*), and honest caveats.

The data pipeline that produces these results is operated privately. This repository publishes **method and findings only** — not infrastructure, credentials, or any third-party confidential work.

> All studies are research / backtested. No live capital, no audited track record. Metrics are reported as method, with confidence intervals and out-of-sample tests, not as a return stream.

| 01 | [Volume-sweep microstructure](01-volume-sweep-microstructure/) | When several chip stocks light up with huge volume at once, does that predict the next day, and is it bigger in small caps? | Clean null at every size. I split a 39-name semiconductor universe into four market-cap buckets (Small <$10B / Mid $10-100B / Large $100-500B / Mega >$500B); no sweep type beats its own basket's next-day drift once I count honestly, correct for multiplicity, benchmark vs random entry, and split out-of-sample. The 'bigger effect in smaller names' hunch shows up only as a point estimate in the smallest bucket's MIXED cell (+1.40%) and it's five wild days: insignificant against drift (p=0.31), blowing up with the threshold and out-of-sample. Size scales the noise, not the signal. |
| 02 | [IPO-anchored VWAP](02-ipo-anchored-vwap/) | Does buying the IPO-anchored VWAP touch actually work, and does it depend on company size? | No edge, and netted against the cohort it's slightly worse than random. Across 881 IPOs / 188 liquid names the touch wins ~49% (90d CI [43.0, 54.2]); the big positive averages are cohort drift (random entry made +144% at 1y vs the touch's +25%), and on a clustered test the touch underperforms random by 22pts at 90d (significant at 1y, p=0.026). It holds out-of-sample and survives no size bucket or refinement; the cohort is all small caps (none above $100B). |
| 03 | [Due-diligence hub → Tempus tracker](03-due-diligence-news-hub/) | Can mixed-reliability sources become a verified, refreshable single-name read? *(tracked name: Tempus, TEM)* | A **living** single-name tracker: Tempus price / drawdown / relative-strength since IPO + a sourced idea ledger (Signatera-MRD adoption, biopharma data-licensing, path to EBITDA breakeven) grounded in the note record. Refreshable; honest that social coverage is nil and news thin. |
| 04 | [License-driven crypto-infra M&A](04-license-driven-crypto-ma/) | Do the GENIUS/CLARITY milestones move the licensed crypto cohort, and do acquirers buy the licence stack rather than build it? | No, and no-as-stated. The cohort's ~17% beta-adjusted drop after the July-2025 milestones is indistinguishable from a random 21-day window for these names (empirical p ~0.13 vs naive 0.02); cohort beta runs 1.2-4.4 so the old 'abnormal vs SPY' was mostly leverage; and the regulated stack is built in-house about as often as it's bought, retiring 'buy not build'. The deal record ($6.0bn / 8 disclosed completed) is a landscape, not a trade. |
| 05 | [News-sentiment signal](05-news-sentiment-signal/) | Does news sentiment predict forward returns, or just react to past ones — and does it depend on company size? | Reactive, and the mirror is sharpest in small caps. Across 9,762 model-tagged posts on 211 names split into 4 cap buckets, sentiment tracks the prior 20-day return (clustered IC +0.145) ~3x more strongly than the matched forward 20-day (+0.049), and that backward link falls monotonically with size (Small +0.244 to Mega +0.065). The forward flicker is mostly momentum in disguise (+0.049 to +0.010 once momentum is removed) and the tradable long-short spread is statistically zero (+0.38%, CI [-0.45%, +1.19%]). A momentum mirror, not an entry signal. |

| 07 | [Intraday decision-time](07-intraday-overnight-decomposition/) | How early in the day can you call the close — and can you trade it? | Conditional. By 11:30 ET the morning move calls the close 79% of the time, but ~69 of those points are pure overlap arithmetic; the leftover gap is a real continuation edge worth +5.4 bp gross (CI [+1.1,+10.0], clears zero on 5/5 seeds, stronger out-of-sample, biggest in small caps). After realistic costs only the >$500B megacap bucket survives, at +1.8 bp net. |
| 08 | [AI model-launch event study](08-ai-model-launch-event-study/) | When a frontier AI model ships, does the software complex actually move? | No. Across 113 software names and 11 launches the group's reaction sits at the 52nd percentile of a random-date placebo; the famous GPT-4 "crash" was the SVB banking week (-6.96% beta-1 cuts to -2.74% on a market model); the disruption gap (exposed vs insulated) reconciles to roughly zero with CIs on zero, and no cap bucket clears zero. The only consistent signal is relative: software beat semis in 7 of 10 launches (+1.07 pts avg), CI still on zero -- a pair to study, not a trade. |
| 09 | [Activist short post-performance](09-activist-short-post-performance/) | After an activist short report, does the stock keep falling — and does it matter who published it or how big the target is? | No tradable post-report drift, in any size bucket. Across 96 campaigns (14 publishers, 2020-26) the 21-day path is indistinguishable from a random day in the same names (edge +1.8pp, dependence-adjusted CI spans 0; the mean even runs positive, the wrong sign for a short), and the small-minus-large cap difference is flat (-0.4pp). Net of borrow the short edge is negative at every horizon and ~20% of campaigns squeezed >+20%. The only structure is the publisher, and the forensic-shops 'win' is one Muddy Waters bounce cohort (pull it out and forensic targets fall -10.7%) — real-looking but small-sample and squeeze-driven, so Conditional, not a validated edge. |
| 10 | [Korea memory cycle + leverage](10-korea-memory-cycle-leverage/) | After a sharp drop, does the levered Korea memory trade spiral down or bounce — and is that anything Korea-specific? | No spiral in the proxy — it rebounds, and the rebound isn't Korean. Across 7 chip names (Korea proxy EWY + US memory MU/WDC/STX + like-vol US ETFs SMH/SOXX/XSD, 2,525 days, 2016-2026) a worst-decile drop is followed by *higher* returns at every horizon (mean spread +1.37%), strongest and only-significant in the deepest tail (worst-2.5%: 21d +4.8% [+2.0,+7.9], 63d +8.3% [+1.5,+14.2]) — the wrong sign for a margin cascade. But all 7 names bounce the same; Korea only clears zero because it's the calmest (27% vol vs 42-50% for single names), so its CIs are tightest. Against a like-vol US ETF the edge nearly vanishes (21d +1.14% vs +0.76%; the old 'half as deep' drawdown was a vol artifact). Verdict Conditional: no spiral in what's measurable, but the clean levered Samsung/SK Hynix tape is data-blocked. |
| 11 | [Semiconductor concentration](11-semiconductor-concentration/) | How much of a chip index is one stock, and do this year's winners win again? | On an 82-name from-scratch index, NVDA is 43% of the gain and 32% of weight; the top three are 79% and the seven names over $500B are 91% of all gain. Cap-weighted +489% vs equal +296% (a 193pt gap), more concentrated than the Nasdaq on 86% of days. Persistence is not testable annually (only 2 clean year-transitions) and flat at the monthly/quarterly horizon (IC +0.02-0.03, t<1.3). A risk fact, not a timing signal. |
| 12 | [GOOGL — laggard or not](12-googl-five-year-hold/) | Was Google really a laggard, and what drove the 2025-26 re-rating? | Never the laggard. On clean prices GOOGL returned +148% (2nd of 6, beats 4 of 5 megacap peers, loses only to NVDA); the "laggard" was a 23x data-corruption bug plus benchmark choice. Of five named catalysts only two survive (Gemini 2.0 and DOJ remedies); the "leader-phase alpha" is not significant once the start date is exogenous (Newey-West t=1.15, bootstrap CI crosses 0). The +148% is ~62% earnings / 38% multiple — a delivery story, not a re-rate. |
| 13 | [Gold vs Bitcoin: collapse insurance?](13-gold-collapse-insurance/) | When equities collapse, does gold protect — and is Bitcoin "digital gold" or a risk asset? | Bitcoin is a risk asset, not digital gold. Across 8 SPY selloffs (≥8%) gold beat the market in 7/8 (median −3%) and Bitcoin fell worse in 7/8 (median −29%) — both significant (sign p=0.035; Wilcoxon p=0.008/0.012). Bitcoin's daily beta to SPY is 0.94 (1.68 in the worst-5% tail, HAC t=7.2/3.1) and its 90-day SPY correlation rose 0.08→0.37, the later half higher in 107/107 split points (trend t=7.2). Gold is the better cushion but not an always-up haven (positive tail beta +0.37). |
| 14 | [Alphabet's investment book](14-alphabet-investment-portfolio/) | Is Alphabet actually a skilled investor versus its peers? | Best cost basis, no detectable picking skill. Alphabet got the cheapest megacap entry into two generational private winners (SpaceX, Anthropic; ~40–55× vs ~18× for Microsoft, ~9× for Amazon; +$24bn ≈ 18% of FY25 net income, but the same line lost money in 2022). On the public CapitalG/GV names I can actually price (22 of them vs a 1,078-name same-era IPO control), the backed basket shows no edge at any horizon once tested with a name-level bootstrap on the difference — and what looked like an edge is listing vintage: the control swings the same way. |
| 15 | [IPO chase and six-month price-action model](15-ipo-chase/) | Do new listings pay off if you buy in the aftermarket, and can that base rate support a reusable six-month IPO scorecard? | Broad aftermarket IPO chasing is a base-rate loser: 760 US IPOs since 2022-10 had a day-1-close one-year median excess return of -28.4% versus SPY, and only 19% beat the index. The RIA equity-supply extension adds nuance: IPO waves can be late-cycle warnings, but IPO count alone is not proof of broad-market greed or high multiples; from 1960-2025, IPO count has only a +0.077 Spearman correlation with CAPE. |
| 16 | [Narrow leadership & the index](16-narrow-leadership-and-the-index/) | When mega-caps carry the index and breadth thins, is that bearish — and can you time it? | No warning survives. Across % above 200/50-DMA, the A/D line, new-highs-minus-lows, McClellan and cap-vs-equal momentum, the signs run the wrong way (high breadth weakly precedes lower returns), every conditional 63-day difference is bootstrap-insignificant, and every cash-on-warning overlay trails buy-and-hold on total return out-of-sample (the two that improve Sharpe give up return and fail robustness). The lone residue (% above 200-DMA de-risking) is one-crash evidence. |
| 17 | [Semiconductor supply-chain layers](17-semiconductor-layers/) | Which layer leads, and how much of the lead is just owning chips? | Completing the roster to 143 names barely moves the leaderboard (Spearman rho 0.962; only EMS re-ranks, by dilution). On raw return 6 of 13 layers beat the semis ETF (SMH), but strip out chip-beta with a Newey-West regression and 0 of 13 has significant alpha (best t=1.9) — the lead is leveraged semiconductor beta, not an edge. The one real cross-sectional pattern is size: mid-caps ($10-100B) returned +938% (47% beat SMH) while small-caps (<$10B) lagged at +244% (8% beat SMH). |
| 18 | [Computex event study](18-computex-event-study/) | Do the chip names that headline or supply Computex beat the semis tape after the keynote? | No robust alpha — abnormal vs SMH is statistically zero at every horizon (0 of 40 cells significant) and flips sign across robustness cuts (drop-2021 +1.2%, pre-2021 listings −2.4%); the median name beats SMH less than half the time. Own SMH, not the basket. |
| 19 | [Shorting the semis](19-shorting-the-semis/) | Does conditioning a semiconductor short on "overbought" (RSI>70, >15% above 200-DMA, +20%/21d) beat shorting blind, and does the answer depend on size? | No. Across 68 chip names over ten years the overbought short earns a negative edge over a blind short at every horizon (5/21/63/126d: -0.15 / -0.40 / -1.93 / -4.29pp). Size doesn't save it (negative for >$500B and <$10B; the only positive bucket is 4 names); a genuinely selective filter (all-three, 3.5% of days) is the worst short of all; the null is worse out of sample and unchanged by borrow cost. Overbought is a melt-up warning, not a short signal. |
| 20 | [Sector rotation](20-sector-rotation/) | Does money rotate with the market cycle — by sector and by size — can you trade it, and which slice is worth owning? | Three cuts, one lesson. By sector, rotation is real and large (defensives beat the market +15-20pp annualized in bears, cyclicals lead bulls, energy off-axis) but a causal 200-DMA rotation loses to buy-and-hold (0.61 vs 0.77 Sharpe, PBO 94%). By size there is no rotation at all - pure symmetric beta, and the 'defensive small-cap' reading is a calendar artifact. You can't time it either way. The usable edge is which slice to own: across 253 names in equal-count cap quartiles, investability rises with size in a clean line (+0.31 Sharpe per 10x cap, corr 0.60; median name Sharpe -0.25 smallest to +0.52 largest; 14% to 71% investable), and a static long top-half / short bottom-half tilt earned the best risk-adjusted return in-sample (Sharpe 1.29) - a regime-specific quality factor bet, not a timing signal. |
| 21 | [A semiconductor risk-overlay model](21-semis-risk-model/) | Can a simple risk dial beat just holding the chips, or does it only make the ride smoother? | It only makes the ride smoother. Across 36 chip stocks (2022-26, ~5y daily) a trend dial that halves net-long when the basket loses its 200-day average or falls >10% off its 60-day high cuts the worst drawdown from -43% to -30% full-sample, and it does so in every market-cap bucket (small caps -51% to -38%, mega caps -46% to -34%) so the protection is not a mega-cap artifact. But the Sharpe edge over buy-and-hold is +0.07 with a bootstrap CI of [-0.20, +0.35] that straddles zero, and the scorecard de-risk value is -4pp (the wrong sign for a timer). Verdict: a validated drawdown-reducer, not alpha. Robust across nine floor/MA settings (Sharpe 1.18-1.29); the one honest dent is the 2023 chop, where whipsaw made drawdown 2pp worse. |
| 22 | [Equity issuance as a market-top signal](22-equity-issuance-top-signal/) | Does a wave of equity issuance (IPOs + secondary offerings) mark an overpriced market and predict weak forward returns? | No usable timing signal — across 2001–2026 (Nasdaq Composite + SPY/QQQ/RSP) with survivorship-free EDGAR filing counts, 0 of 12 non-overlapping annual tests are significant. A faint IPO/SPAC froth tilt survives in direction (strongest in tech) but is ~half momentum and 2020–22-bound; the "SEOs invert" result is an EDGAR coverage-ratio artifact; mega-caps are net repurchasers. The real effect is cross-sectional (study 15), not market timing. |
| 23 | [Robotics closed-end fund vs MSTR premium replay](23-robotics-fund-mstr-premium-replay/) | If a robotics closed-end fund trades at a persistent private-access premium and uses a MicroStrategy-style issuance flywheel, do early public investors benefit? | Conditional. | 26 | [Election money flows](26-election-money-flows/) | Around U.S. elections, does money actually leave the market or the country - and what do Trump wins, midterms, and wars each do to flows, returns, and volatility? | The folklore is half right. The hedging cycle is real (median VIX builds into the vote, collapses to ~15 within a week of any result; cash builds pre-vote) but money then re-risks: U.S. equity funds take a median +6.5% of assets in the 13 weeks after the vote, 6 of 6 since 2004. "Capital flees America" is not in the flows - after both Trump wins the exit is from China funds (-14.3% of assets in 13 weeks 2016, -20.0% 2024 reaching -36.3% by 26 weeks) while U.S. funds take inflows, Taiwan/Korea lag the U.S. by 12-16 pts at 60 days (2024), and both times leadership flipped to Europe by day 120. Wars fit the war puzzle in 11 of 17 events (telegraphed conflicts sag into outbreak then gain +3.3% median in the month after; surprises bite and fade; war-spiked oil round-trips within a month). Administration volatility is episodic, not ambient: Trump I ran 21% annualized vs Biden 16%, clustered on tariff headlines, with Liberation Day -3.8% in five sessions then the largest one-day gain since 2008 on the pause. Extended June 2026: 25 elections and 18 presidents since 1926 (best Sharpe Eisenhower 1.29; midterm effect 22 of 24; flip elections crush vol 10 of 12), gold accelerating to +29% annualized post-2024 vs a +16% central-bank-era baseline, and defense as the century's most R-sensitive industry with Trump-win flows rotating to materials/defense. |
| 27 | [The AI capital cycle](27-ai-capital-cycle/) | Is the 2024-26 AI buildout a capital cycle in its money-burning phase, held together by circular vendor financing - and where on the historical clock are we? | Mid-to-late frenzy, and the tell is positional. "Losing money" is true only precisely: serving a token is gross-positive (OpenAI ~33%, Anthropic ~40% GM) but the flat-rate heavy-tail subscriber is negative by construction and the all-in burn is a choice until commitments make it contractual (OpenAI ~$1.15T of compute commitments vs ~$13B 2025 revenue). The financing crossed cash -> equity -> debt/SPV in 2025-26, the same progression that preceded the 1847 and 2000 tops; a Greenwood-Shleifer-You >100% two-year-net-of-market screen flags 8 of 17 AI names (MU +704, PLTR +529, GEV +425, INTC +221, VRT +207, AMD +154, AVGO +144, TSM +122) but NOT Nvidia (+34), with MSFT (-35) and META (-8) already negative - the mania migrated downstream to memory/power/application while the bellwether shows no blow-off. The break trigger is a financing refusal, not demand collapse; fragility concentrates in the leveraged periphery (CoreWeave-tier neoclouds -> Oracle as the credit channel -> OpenAI funding gap) while TSMC, FCF-rich hyperscalers and the monopoly chokepoints survive. Every analog (railways 1840s, telecom 1999, radio 1929) separates "the technology is real" from "the capital is safe" - both true at once each time. Positional, not directional: own the toll-road survivors, not "short AI." |

## Method notes

- **Validation:** out-of-sample / walk-forward splits, bootstrap confidence intervals, Spearman information coefficient, hit-rate, t-tests with reported p-values; baseline (random-entry) comparisons so market drift is never mistaken for signal.
- **Honesty first:** null and decayed results are published, not hidden — most of the studies here are nulls. A signal only graduates to "interesting" after it survives a larger, out-of-sample test.
- **Reproducibility:** each note states its universe, window, thresholds, and metric so the result can be challenged.
